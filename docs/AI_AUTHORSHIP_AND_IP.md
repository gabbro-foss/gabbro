# AI Authorship and IP — Notes for Gabbro

## Reference

Sena Evren, *Who Owns the Code Claude Wrote?*, Legal Layer, 28 April 2026.
https://legallayer.substack.com/p/who-owns-the-claude-code-wrote

---

## Summary of the article

Copyright only protects work created by a human. The US Copyright Office and
the DC Circuit (Thaler case, cert denied March 2026) have confirmed this
consistently. Code generated by an AI without meaningful human authorship may
be uncopyrightable — sitting in the public domain in everything but name.

The phrase that determines protection is **"meaningful human authorship"**,
which courts interpret as evidence that a human made genuine creative decisions:

- choosing the architecture
- deciding what to reject
- restructuring output to fit a specific design

Specifying an objective to the model is not enough. Directing how the work is
constructed is what counts.

Three things are settled law:

1. Works lacking human authorship are uncopyrightable.
2. The work-for-hire doctrine applies regardless of how code was generated.
3. Verbatim copying of GPL-licensed code violates the licence.

Two things remain unsettled (no definitive court ruling yet):

1. How much human direction is enough to establish meaningful authorship in an
   agentic workflow.
2. Whether AI output that reproduces training data patterns counts as verbatim
   copying for GPL purposes.

## Assessment for Gabbro

### Where Gabbro is in a strong position

The article's own test — did a human make genuine creative decisions about
architecture? — points firmly at yes for Gabbro as a whole.

The following were human decisions, not AI suggestions:

- The security boundary: secrets never cross the Flutter/Rust bridge in plaintext
- The crypto stack: Argon2id → ML-KEM-1024 → AES-256-GCM
- The hybrid PQC approach (belt and suspenders rationale)
- The session model (Rust owns decrypted vault in memory; Flutter never holds it)
- The GPL-3.0-only licence and the reasoning behind it
- The FIDO2/YubiKey-only authentication model (no TOTP)
- The vault file format design
- The importer scope decision (three importers, not N)
- Numerous design bugs caught independently during development

The ADRs, `ARCHITECTURE.md`, and commit messages are exactly
the documentation the article says establishes meaningful human authorship.
They predate the generated code and show the architect specifying structure
before the AI built it.

### Where it is genuinely ambiguous

Some functions were written by Claude and copied verbatim without modification.
Under current doctrine, those specific functions may not be copyrightable by
anyone. The article is honest that this middle ground is unresolved — no court
has issued a definitive ruling on AI-assisted code specifically.

### Where GPL-3.0 changes the calculus

The article's GPL contamination risk — AI output reproducing training data from
GPL-licensed code — is structurally irrelevant to Gabbro. Gabbro is already
GPL-3.0-only by deliberate choice. Even a worst-case contamination scenario
does not change Gabbro's licence obligations or users' rights. The risk the
article warns commercial proprietary projects about simply does not apply here.

## On the broader question

The article frames this as an AI-specific legal problem. In practice, the same
questions arise in any close human collaboration: a contractor, a pair
programmer, an employee — all raise questions about who owns what. The law has
handled those cases for decades through work-for-hire doctrine, contractor
agreements, and employment contracts.

AI collaboration sits in a grey area today not because the underlying questions
are new, but because the legal frameworks have not yet caught up with the speed
of the technology. The honest answer is that the individual functions Claude
drafted verbatim are the grey area; the architecture, the decisions, and the
project as a whole are not.

The most durable protection is the one already in place: thorough documentation
of human creative decisions, maintained as a matter of good engineering
practice rather than as a legal hedge.
